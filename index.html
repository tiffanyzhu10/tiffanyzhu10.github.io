What's Not on the Résumé

Evaluating COVID-19 Relief in the City of Milpitas

Sustainable Cities (project-based course) • September 2020 – November 2020

In Fall 2020, I took a service-learning course in Stanford's Urban Studies department focusing on the development of sustainable cities. As part of the course, not only did students attend lectures and complete readings, we also partnered with community-based organizations to help them address issues pertaining to sustainability. I joined a team that partnered with the City of Milpitas to evaluate a program they established to help small businesses weather the COVID-induced recession. We presented our initial findings to the Economic Development and Trade Commission on November 12, and wrote a final report that can be downloaded here.

I loved both the class and the project. Through the project, I learned what roles government can play to support the economy, and I got to better know a city that I live just a few miles from. Meanwhile, through the class, I learned about the many dimensions of sustainability, including not just environmental sustainability but also socioeconomic sustainability and the intersection of environmental issues with social justice. I was very used to analyzing social issues through an economic lens; this class broadened my way of thinking about social issues.

Designing and Building a Virtual Study Abroad Experience

Voronezh – New Horizons • July 2020

In summer 2020, I planned to intern at a think tank in Ukraine, but unfortunately, my internship was cancelled due to COVID. Nevertheless, I was determined to find a way to keep participating in international exchange. I discovered that study abroad organization Crossroads Eurasia was organizing a monthlong virtual international collaboration (like a hackathon). I signed up and wound up having one of the most transformative international experiences in my undergraduate career (without even going to Russia!).

The virtual exchange, entitled "Voronezh – New Horizons", brought together Russian students from the city of Voronezh and English-speaking students to solve problems related to cultural exchange. I was assigned to a team with two Russian students and another American student. We were tasked with collaborating to solve a problem related to U.S.-Russian cooperation. Additionally, we were asked not to work on overtly political subjects.

Though this was an unusual constraint, we worked around it to address the problem of cultural immersion in the age of social distancing. We created a website that hosted a virtual tour of Voronezh as well as educational dialogues designed to reinforce a beginner's knowledge of Russian. The collaboration was not without its challenges: we all lived in different time zones, our collaboration took place almost entirely in Russian, and the Russian and American students had different experiences with work culture. Nevertheless, I loved getting to know my teammates and transcending the limitations of COVID to create something new together.

Translating Russian Rock Music

Post-Soviet Dispatches (personal blog) • March 2020 – May 2020

In my free time, I translate Soviet and Russian rock songs into English. When quarantine began, I started to post my translations online along with accompanying notes on the songs' historical and cultural context. I wound up posting 46 translations from March to May. My favorite translations were the fourteen that I did to commemorate the anniversary of one of my favorite albums, Метель августа (August Blizzard) by DDT. As part of this mini-project, I wrote an essay analyzing this album as a whole.

Publishing Articles on Soviet and Russian History and Culture

Russian Life • February 2019 – December 2019

In Winter 2019, I took a course taught by Alice Underwood, a then-Ph.D. student who contributed articles on Russian politics to a magazine called Russian Life. Alice suggested that I apply for an open "Digital Editor Intern" position at the magazine, so I did. Little did I know that my responsibilities would far exceed the humble title of "Digital Editor Intern" — it would involve significant amounts of original research, news research, and content creation.

Every week, I took turns writing up the magazine's weekly newsletter, The Weekly Russia File (fondly abbreviated as TWERF), and writing longer-form articles on Russian history or culture. TWERF was dedicated to covering unusual news stories from Russia, so I enjoyed diving into less-publicized but still fascinating sides of Russian life. Even more than that, though, I loved brainstorming and writing the long-form articles. I had the freedom to write on pretty much any topic I wanted, so long as I informed my editor in time. Topics I wrote on included a snapshot of New York City's Russian immigrant community from the perspective of a Chinese-American; the history of bakeries in the Soviet Union; an analysis of portrayals of the Motherland in Russian rock music; and an article explaining the popularity of Georgian food in Russia. My most popular article was the one on Georgian food, but my favorite to write was the one on the Motherland in Russian rock music.

I am incredibly grateful to have written for Russian Life. My experience there taught me discipline and a willingness to explore subjects beyond my comfort zone. Paul Richardson, my editor, ingrained in me an appreciation for brevity and the process of rewriting and redrafting. Finally, I like to think that writing for TWERF sharpened my sense of humor, but you can be the judge of that.

Traveling and Conducting Research in St. Petersburg

Bing Overseas Seminar Program • September 2018

The summer between my freshman and sophomore years, I spent three weeks living and studying in St. Petersburg as part of a Stanford study abroad program. As part of the seminar, I conducted independent research on the socioeconomic development of a neighborhood in Fyodor Dostoevsky's Crime and Punishment. I looked at occupations and approximate income levels of individuals in the neighborhood from Dostoevsky's times to Soviet and contemporary times. As part of my research, I wrote three blog posts documenting my findings. This seminar was my first taste of independent research and inspired me to take more courses in Russian history and language.
